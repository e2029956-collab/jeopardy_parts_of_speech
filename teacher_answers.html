Parts of Speech
$100
Stative verb
Stative verbs describe states, conditions, or situations that don't change, unlike dynamic verbs that show actions.
$200
Attributive adjective
Attributive adjectives can only modify nouns directly (the main issue) and cannot follow linking verbs (*the issue is main).
$300
Conjunction
Conjunctions join grammatical elements. Coordinating conjunctions connect equal elements, while subordinating conjunctions connect dependent to independent clauses.
$400
Relative pronoun
Relative pronouns connect relative clauses to main clauses and refer back to a noun or pronoun in the main clause (antecedent).
$500
Conjunctive adverb
Conjunctive adverbs show relationships between independent clauses and are typically preceded by semicolons and followed by commas.
Punctuation
$100
Semicolon
Semicolons separate independent clauses when using conjunctive adverbs: 'I studied hard; however, the test was difficult.'
$200
Oxford comma / Serial comma
The Oxford (serial) comma appears before the final conjunction in a list: 'red, white, and blue' vs. 'red, white and blue'.
$300
Colon
Colons introduce lists, explanations, or quotations after complete independent clauses: 'I need three things: paper, pen, and patience.'
$400
Commas
Restrictive (defining) clauses are essential to meaning and don't use commas: 'Students who study pass' vs. 'Students, who study, pass.'
$500
Comma splice
Comma splices incorrectly join independent clauses with just a comma. Use semicolons, periods, or coordinating conjunctions instead.
Verb Forms
$100
Gerund
Gerunds are verb forms ending in -ing that function as nouns. They can be subjects, objects, or complements in sentences.
$200
Present perfect
Present perfect connects past actions to the present moment: 'I have finished my homework' (it's done now).
$300
Past participle
Past participles are used in perfect tenses, passive voice, and as adjectives: 'broken window', 'I have written', 'It was made'.
$400
Third conditional
Third conditional expresses hypothetical past situations: 'If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.'
$500
Infinitive
Infinitives can be 'to + base form' or bare infinitives (without 'to') after modals, causative verbs, and perception verbs.
Modals
$100
Must
Must expresses strong logical deduction: 'She must be home' (the lights are on, car is there - very likely true).
$200
Should have
Should have + past participle expresses regret or criticism: 'I should have studied harder' (but I didn't).
$300
Could
Could shows past ability ('I could swim at age 5'), present possibility ('It could rain'), and polite requests ('Could you help?').
$400
Have to
Have to expresses obligation or necessity from external circumstances: 'I have to work tomorrow' (it's required).
$500
Cannot / Can't
Cannot/couldn't have + past participle expresses impossibility in the past: 'She can't have finished already' (it's impossible).
Sentence Structure
$100
Dangling modifier
Dangling modifiers create confusion: 'Walking down the street, the trees looked beautiful' (trees can't walk!).
$200
Parallel structure / Parallelism
Parallel structure maintains consistency: 'I like reading, writing, and studying' (all gerunds) not 'reading, to write, and study'.
$300
Complex sentence
Complex sentences combine independent and dependent clauses: 'Although it was raining, we went outside.'
$400
Independent clause
Independent clauses express complete thoughts: 'The sun is shining' can stand alone as a sentence.
$500
Compound sentence
Compound sentences join equal clauses with conjunctions or semicolons: 'I studied hard, and I passed the test.'
Common Mistakes
$100
Greengrocer's apostrophe
This common error adds apostrophes to simple plurals. Use apostrophes only for possession (cat's toy) or contractions (it's).
$200
It's
'Its' shows possession (the dog wagged its tail), while 'it's' means 'it is' or 'it has' (it's raining).
$300
Fewer
Use 'fewer' with countable nouns (fewer books) and 'less' with uncountable nouns (less water).
$400
John and I
Use subject pronouns in subject position: 'John and I went' not 'Me and John went.' Remove the other person to test: 'I went.'
$500
Split infinitive
Split infinitives place adverbs between 'to' and the verb. While often acceptable in modern English, formal writing may prefer 'to go boldly.'
